## The Immortal Jellyfish: How Turritopsis dohrnii Effectively Hits the 'Reset' Button on Life

Did you know there’s a jellyfish that might be biologically immortal? It’s true! *Turritopsis dohrnii*, a tiny species found in oceans worldwide, possesses a remarkable ability to revert to its polyp stage – essentially hitting a biological reset button. This incredible feat has captivated scientists and offers profound insights into the nature of aging and regeneration.

### The Secret to Immortality: Transdifferentiation

Unlike most animals that age and die, *Turritopsis dohrnii* can transform back into its juvenile polyp form when faced with physical damage or starvation. This process, called transdifferentiation, involves reverting differentiated cells back to an earlier, less specialized state. It’s not true immortality in the sense of being invulnerable; rather, it's a remarkable escape from death by aging.

#### The Life Cycle of a Turritopsis dohrnii

The life cycle of *Turritopsis dohrnii* is quite unique:

1.  **Medusa Stage:** The jellyfish lives as a free-swimming medusa, reproducing sexually to produce larvae.
2.  **Planula Larva:** The larvae settle on the seabed and develop into polyps.
3.  **Polyp Stage:** Polyps are small, stalk-like structures that reproduce asexually, creating clones of themselves.
4.  **Transdifferentiation:** When stressed, the polyp can transform back into a juvenile medusa, restarting the cycle.

### Evolutionary Marvel

The ability of *Turritopsis dohrnii* to revert to its polyp stage is an evolutionary marvel. This unique mechanism provides a survival advantage in fluctuating environments. By reverting to a less demanding life stage when conditions become unfavorable, the jellyfish can avoid death and continue its life cycle. This resilience is a testament to the power of adaptation.

### Conservation Status

Currently, *Turritopsis dohrnii* is listed as **Least Concern** by the IUCN (International Union for Conservation of Nature). While widespread, they are susceptible to habitat changes and pollution.
